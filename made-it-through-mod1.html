Made it through Mod1!

April 28, 2017

Back on March 13, I started the first 6-week module at Turing School of Software and Design, and it has turned out to be one of the most challenging and rewarding experiences in my life. I was drawn to Turing based on their mission and my first-hand experience at a Try Turing weekend. The program is broken down into 4 6-week modules, with the first module being focused on Ruby and learning the basics of being a software engineer. In Mod1 I completed several individual projects as well as two paired projects, which incorporated a LOT of learning around the workflow tool Git.

My favorite technical concept I from the first module was the understanding of how ANYTHING can be abstracted into code, and especially the flexibility of this in Object Oriented Programming. I come to Turing with 8 years experience as a technical medical device engineer, where I worked in manufacturing to improve processes, reduce/eliminate defect rates and reduce cost of the product. While I learned a tremendous amount about medical devices and how manufacturing/design can directly affect patient outcome, almost all the defects and issues could be traced back to human error. Whether it was a less than optimal design that failed to address all possible failure modes, or simply a person making an error when following a procedure, almost every root cause analysis was a result of a human being human.

Last year I found out the Colorado branch of the company I worked at was being shut down and I would be laid off about the same time google announced they had defeated a master in the game of Go. I had also recently learned about Merck and other pharmaceutical companies utilizing machine learning to find the best drug molecules. It occurred to me how medical device companies might use similar programs to identify and fix potential failure modes to improve design and reduce the probability of a poor patient outcome. Even with just my basic understanding of programming I am starting to see how this could work, and how I might be able to use this technology someday if I keep learning and progressing. Now that we can model, test and simulate use of anything digitally, I am very excited for the future and combining software with medical devices to help patients!
